Chapter One: Universal Living Communities

Friday, 6:19 AM

June 16th

 

 

"JAYNE!" Her mother's voice echoed again, drowning out in the waters of her dreams, slowly breaching it's way to the surface.

She was dreaming of an ocean. Floating romantically in the soft salt smell of the waves— her mind lost in the calls of seagulls, the sunlit waters mist tickling her nose...

Although, this was all in theory, of course, and that was only what she imagined an ocean would smell, and be like. See, she had never been to a real ocean in person— but somehow in her dreams, she seemed to emulate things in that way, expertly so. Recapturing the mere, careful imagery that displayed itself inside the books she so desperately clung to. Bringing it to life. And that seemed to suit her cruel, longing ache for the outside world— for now, at least—

"Now, Jayne!"

Her mind stuttered awake again, this time, her head more fervently above water, "I gave you an extra twenty minutes, come down right now or you're going to be late!"

She heard that more clearly indeed.

Wait...

Wake up.

Jayne Naya Jeevan slowly opened her eyes. Then strained her head up slightly from her pillow, turning over to face the ceiling. She pinched her thumb and forefinger between her brow, blinking long as reality settled in.

Why must she leave her mind for this place...

She heaved a breath, kicking her long, dark legs out from the tangled white sheets, sliding over to sit on the edge of her bed. She squinted across the room, focusing up at the hanging, wooden clock opposite her bed, and then froze her vision in sustained relief.

Oh, 6:20, she had time? 

Her mother could be so dramatic. See, she planned to go to the Schoolhouse a bit earlier today to return her textbooks... but class didn’t start for almost another two whole hours... and wait a minute? A sudden, strong whiff of... something blinded her mind in a different direction.

Breakfast meat...? That’s strange.

Oh, but now she could absolutely pinpoint the source to that salty “sea air” of her dreams. Directly to the distracting smell of Crackling Bacon down in the kitchen...

She stretched up her arms towards the ceiling and smiled with the welcoming notion that it must be paired with Buttered Toast... and hopefully Simmering Sausage. All such oddly normalized motivation... and it was all waiting for her downstairs—  But just as quickly then, a worrying thought snuck into her head, suddenly halting her to pause a moment in curiosity.

Wait, the Nutrition Schedule didn’t list that for today, though? Something wasn’t right.

"Jayne!"

Ugh. The nag! "Okayyy, I'm com—" she sing-songed loudly enough for her mother to hear from downstairs. But her voice was sticky, considering she hadn’t spoken yet this morning, so she yelled again, standing up to humour her, “I will be down in a moment.”

So today was Friday...

But they weren't supposed to supply bacon on Friday’s in this month’s calendar? 

She padded across her sand-white carpet to cross another day off of her calendar.

There it was, Friday the sixteenth— and under Nutrition, it only listed, Savory Spinach Omelettes, Rye Toast with Pomegranate Jam, and Daily Essentials (Coffee, Orange Juice, Milk and Water). 

See, her family, and all the neighboring families in her community, received weekly by monthly meal plans for specific days. And today's, was wholesomely incorrect. ...Unless her nose had betrayed her, and then she was imagining this whole crisis for no reason at all.

"Jayne!"

She groaned, placing the marker back on the wall shelf. Maybe this was on account for the special occasion, for everyone involved?

For graduation?

...Oh well. Bacon was downstairs. Jayne reminded herself, well, still hoping she was right...

She slipped on her slippers and bumbled wildly down the winding staircase— not even bothering to check her appearance. She knew she probably looked like how she felt. Chaotic, hungry, and of course, an absolute mess. 

Her normally shiny raven hair was knotted up on top of her head. Dull waves cascading down the sides that had fallen loose over her dark olive blushed cheeks. She knew well that her ancestry related back to India (a country from a long time ago that was apparently inhabited by people who looked like her and her family.) And this was why her, and her brother's skin color were so much darker than everyone else's at school— well, almost everyone.

See, her town, Pinecone-Paeonia, like every other town she knew existed, had been perfectly organized into sectioned housing units of individual families by culture. Universal Living Communities, were what they were called. Yes, ULC’s, for short, were a perfect containment of generously spaced, pastel colored, two-story houses for every family. Everything they needed was either delivered, or in walking or biking distance. 

Oh, and more importantly, the weather of her town was most sincerely spectacular. It consisted of bright, misty, sunny summers, cool crisp autumns, and stone-cold white winters- and that was Jayne’s favorite aspect about her town. She loved the change.

Each town contained one Hospital, supplied with one Ambulance Truck. One Library. One Gym. One Shopping Mall. One Movie Theater. One Restaurant. One Concert Hall. One Labratory. One Art Museum. One Bar. One Salon. One Bowling Alley. One Community Garden. One Football Stadium. One Gymnasium. One Dog Park. One Police Station. One Firehouse. One Schoolhouse, with one School Park Playground, as well as it’s thirteen, separately attached sub buildings for each progressing year.

And every town looked like this.

Though, she was sure that not every town had the special trees and weather like Pinecone-Paeonia (she had read about other towns of extreme, year-round heat in her geography books), she knew for a fact that every town had the exact same resources. And keep in mind, that yes, there were only one of these buildings per town, but each one alone was magnificent in size.

And in each town, like hers, each family’s house, represented a different country. One of each, for lack of a better phrase- in fact, Pinecone-Paeonia had even less of a variety of represented countries than other towns (coming in at only about 11% of all world countries,) as Pinecone-Paeonia was mostly a collection of Eastern Europe. 

To name them all, they were:

Poland, Switzerland, Israel, Ukraine, Romania, Germany, France, Spain, Turkey, Greece, Norway, Scotland, Sweden, Finland, Netherlands, Austria, Italy, Syria, Iceland, England, Iran and Ireland. And then there was her, from India. And then next to her, the other outcast was Kenya. Though she knew India originally was not exactly close to Eastern Europe... somehow they, and Kenya ended up in Pinecone-Paeonia. Because that was equality... and that was diversity. Although she was sure that the two were meant to be different things. And her friend Timothy, and his younger sister Cassidy, from Kenya, actually had much darker skin than her. And unadmittingly so, she did feel slightly less excluded because of their presence. Though that ironically made her feel worse, in a way. And Timothy always knew how to make her laugh.

She wished she could care half as less about her self-image the way Tim could. And their town was a lower populated town, so they needed to have less of a variety... and that was a prospect of equality- by definition, of course. Not by her own opinion. Considering she lived in “Pinecone-Paeonia” and had zero insight as to where that was now or what country that used be a part of.

There were 999,999 other towns all over the world- and she wouldn’t dare try to list them all, but most of them, at least had a larger spectrum of countries (coming in at usually greater than 45%... It wasn’t fair.) But she couldn't complain though. She lived a comfortable life— as well did everyone else. And there was nothing to covet, as it was all... the same.

And although she felt isolated in her own skin, she was still enviously described as classically beautiful by her mother and best friend Diana. Attributing that to her striking, light bearing hazel eyes and strong, symmetrical features— which, was what she thought physical beauty should be depicted by and only.

She was average in height and weight, and had a short, straight nose and high cheekbones. And her dark brows were full— accenting the rounded, yet angular definition of her features. However, unfortunately, and according to many— like a certain, Miss Perfect, Johanna Jensen, if something or someone was different, then it wasn't the same- and if it wasn't the same, then that meant, that it couldn't be perfect.

Perfect... to fit in with the rest of the all too seemingly perfect world she lived in. And perfect she was not.

Chapter Two: Change is the Essential Process of All Existence

Friday, 6:36 AM

June 16th

 

Jayne stepped into the kitchen to see her father reading the newspaper with one hand and sipping a steaming, hot cup of coffee in the other. Under the table was her family’s pet dog, Bongo- a panting, black and chestnut haired German Sheppard. He lifted his head up gently as she walked in.

Their wooden cabinet and white-tile kitchen always felt claustraphobically tidy, although it was more than spacious. However, it was the creaky, oakwood flooring, warmly placed furniture and quietly arranged white marble island, that actually made it bearable and even quite cozy.

Jayne watched as a strip of sunlight drenched a beam of light across the round wooden table that her father was sat at, right in front of the big, square window. She thought that the image as a whole— with the blurry, windowed background of soft green hills and concrete buildings outside— was breath-taking in the moment. Granted, every kitchen looked the way theirs did, but it was moments like this, in which she recognized the tiny specks of dust floating in the sunlight, that reminded Jayne that her home could be special.

"Morning, Jane," her father said hoarsely, and then cleared his throat. "Sleep well?"

"Yes," she stared absently at his black and gray work tie. “Absolutely.”

She then felt his eyes examine her heavily, particularly focusing in on her nose-ring with judgement. He closed his eyes languidly with a small sigh. (Jayne saw an old photograph in a history book once, and since then, she had been forever mesmerized by the picture’s captivating, colorful group of young Indian women. She had never admired a photograph so distinctly. As they all had these elegant, nose-ring chains— and the most beautiful red clothes Jayne had ever seen. So on her sixteenth birthday, she got her nose pierced, like them- with her mother’s permission, of course, and she wore the golden hoop in honor... of her past culture. Her mother saw it as representation. Her father saw it as rebellion. Jayne might admit she saw it as something between the two.) She self-consciously felt over the thin, gold metal that looped through her left nostril.

And if there's something anyone must know, it is that her father didn't approve of Jayne's scandalous personality most of the time. And he especially did not approve of her persistent desire to change her given name.

She could just tell when he was calling her "Jane.”

Oh, how she wished she had a long, beautiful name like one from her ancestry- a name from a time when people spoke languages other than just the Standard. Yes, a name from her books- like Priyanka or Latika, or even just a long, pretty name in general. Like Persephone. And it's not that she hated her real name. Jane. It's just that, Jane... was so utterly plain. And she did not want a name— that she had no choice in deciding, by the way— to define her. Boxing her in to that plain, Jane, boring lifestyle. One that so pathetically advertised itself to her everywhere.

So, all she was asking... was to simply add a "y" in her name. Or to call her "Jay." And her father had sarcastically responded that that was very fitting of her... to add a "y" in, because "why" was a word she used far-terribly too often.

"Father. This breakfast is exquisite," Jayne beamed, plopping down in the seat next to him. He twitched an amused, heavy brow at her, the mug still resting just under his mustache. A weary look started to grow on his face, now dropping the newspaper to the table.

"You know, Jane..." he touched his shadowed-throat gingerly, clearing it again, "You're eighteen. That’s an adult now. Things are going to start to change for you, very fast, and very soon." He pushed up the bridge of his dark maroon, framed glasses, tilting his chin down to look at her more sternly, "You need to start taking on more responsibility for yourself. Your mother shouldn't still be waking you up for school."

Jayne rolled her eyes. "All right. Well, soon after today, she won't have to. I won’t be here."

Yes, after today.

Because today, was the last day of school. And it was the last day, for the last time. The last, last day of Primary school! Oh, the deeply embedded thrill of new possibilities had been lately too overwhelming. 

Her father actually chuckled at that. "Oh... my dear. You worry me." He took a bite of his scrambled eggs, as Jayne's mother stepped back into the kitchen, carrying Jayne's bright yellow schoolbag. 

"You left your essay on the dining room table. I put it back in your folder." Her mother threw a glance at her knowingly. "I’m surprised, Mr. Gellert has you doing work on the last day? "

Hmm. Maybe her father was right...

But where would there be an opportunity, to be responsible at home... when her mother was around?

"Thank you, mother, but I would have seen it, if you had only given me the chance." She then took a long swig of her orange juice, washing down her eggs. "And yes, it’s something he wanted to revisit with our class, but it’s not much. The essay's just about our expectations for College, which we'll all share today." She carefully leaned over the table to fork some sausage and bacon. "We're actually only doing College Preparation today."

"Oh, the final days of College Preparation, how excited everyone must be!" Her mother sighed dreamily, placing a hand on her husband's shoulder. "I can remember when I was still the only person I knew in Oak-Lily Town— besides my parents, of course— that was of Indian descent..." she exhaled again wistfully. "Then I met your father, of course.”

”Of course.” Jayne echoed flatly.

”Yes, well, it just seems like a lifetime ago. Sometimes I think about how lucky we were— chosen, to have another baby when we did, Jayne. Your father and I weren't so lucky growing up."

Her father nodded dutifully, "Yes, it really makes a probable difference, I would imagine. Knowing someone else your age, who comes from the same place." He shrugged to Jayne, easing an easy smile. “It matters immensely.”

Yes, but you and mother both had classmates from Saudi Arabia, Ghana, Sri Lanka, Mali, Iraq, Korea, Haiti, Brazil, Taiwan, Libya, Niger, Africa and Pakistan.  Albeit, much more diverse than here, she thought. It was just her and her brother. They couldn’t even compare to imagine how alone she felt. 

"Though you are still going to see plenty others like you in College. And from towns all over." Her father smirked cheekily to her mother, and then peered back at Jayne. "You know, don't look so surprised, Jane... Because one day, you and your Life Mate are going to be saying the same thing, to your child."

He boomed a deep, hearty laugh at Jayne's blatant look of horror, and then at her mother, as she chimed in with mirth, "Or children, John? We can only hope!"

"All right— Can we not talk about this please?" Jayne said bitterly, swallowing down some more juice. "By the way, why do we have breakfast meat privileges today? It's Friday?" 

"Wow..." Her brother, Samuel said, dragging out the word dryly. He huffed haughtily to their father, strolling into the kitchen through the other doorway. "I bet father my tie you wouldn't have noticed," he chuckled at her teasingly, leaning tall against the wood frame, already dressed in his blue and silver school uniform.

"Well, Ha-ha. I did. So what's the deal?"

Her younger brother only rolled his eyes as her answer, before remarking, “Come on, Jayne.”

She squinted back at him impatiently. Sometimes, Sam could barely even say anything, and the mere sight of his expression would make her angry. See Sam was tall and lean, and had an array of dark curly hair splayed in an even mess across his forehead. He looked older than her, but he still had another year left of Primary School. So his biting tone was just due to his jealousy, she decided.

”Father?” Jayne asked.

Sam glanced lazily at his father when he put down his paper again. And just then, Jayne noticed the big, bold words on the front, title page that made her stomach flutter.

Graduates of Yesterday, Collegates of Tomorrow. A Happy Life Awaits. Goodluck Class of the Present!

"Service gave it to us earlier this week. We thought you’d enjoy it for today. All the graduates received the same complement," her father shook his paper open again to a new page.

The Bad News section.

Jayne had always taken curious notice that this section of the paper was typically vapid. And there was never a time she could remember that the news had ever caused her parents to panic or to remark anything other than, "Oh, well, that was a close one."

Their newspaper covered her and her nine neighboring towns and that was it. Pinecone-Paeonia, Rose-Blossom, Olive-Wood, Pepper-Leaf, Lily-Juniper, Orange-Tree, Oak-Lily, Chestnut-Clover, Cinnamon-Grass and Sage-Vine. They heard nothing ever of the other 999,990 towns that assumingly had the same, low production of Bad News as them. Which was a good thing... because nothing Bad ever happened in the world of today. Ever.

She often wondered the real purpose of a newspaper. Was it to let people know about the Bad Things that... could have happened? But didn't?

Today said:

“Firemen save kitten from tree before fall.”

(It said that almost every other week. And yet still, her mother always smiled with relief, every time.)

“Boy rescued before kidnapping.

Girl stopped by police before attempted theft.

Missing child; precautionary mission successful.

Total accounted arrests: 2”

"That's what I assumed." Jayne smiled, thoughtfully ignoring her dogged, troubling curiosity that so pathetically controlled her thoughts, taking her last bite of sausage. She had been eating rather quickly, as she kind-of began to realize that she didn’t have as much time as she thought. (Time perception was not her strongest attribute, if one could guess.) Her brother sat down in the chair next to her, sliding a plate over. "You are the only person I know to get this fixated over just breakfast."

"It's not just about the breakfast, Sam-Mule." Jayne huffed, "It's about the schedule change.” She peered at her father, and then behind him, at where the window sunbeam once was. It had now shifted over, highlighting their dusty, hanging family portrait. “Change is the essential process of all existence.”

Sam gave her a look. A pretend-sad look, that she knew all too well from him. “Sure...” he nodded, twitching a condescending frown. “Jay, that makes complete sense.”

She mocked a frown back. “Well, I just think that it’s a wonderfully new way to start the day, is all," she exclaimed on a whim, hiccuping with her mouth half-full. “It’s a promising symbol of my future.” She stood up fast, her chair squeaking a wretched sound against the tile floor.

"Jayne, slow down..." Her mother reprimanded, taking the plate from her hands as a precaution. "Now, just go get dressed. I'll clean up for you."

"Thank you! Yes!" She kissed her mother's cheek, skipping then measurably up the hall, only to bound up the staircase when she was out of sight.

She knew this was what her father meant by needing to take on more of her own responsibility. But she supposed there was no harm in taking advantage of these last few weeks that she was home? Before she was really on her own.

How hard could simple tasks really be anyway? Laundry, dishes... cooking? Those things weren't complex or difficult to figure out...

But that's just it. They were easy tasks.

And easy tasks like that, were just such a chore for her.

Though, not to sound all too entirely immature, because it wasn't just that- but it was that she truly, rather enjoyed spending her time and effort, on things that were more important... or interesting to her.

Like dreaming. Or theorizing life in her head. Or reading.

Oh right, could Jayne Naya Jeevan read...

She could read for hours and hours upon hours and not even take notice the time. She once read all of Jane Eyre in just three days! (She was most especially motivated the first time she read it because of her name.) Though on another note to that, they were only allowed Library visits on Monday, Wednesday and Saturday, so she did read it on three separate days altogether, but still... she was proud of herself. Jayne would binge books without sleep if she could, yes.

Her teacher, Mr. Gellert, actually once gave her a book in her ninth year of school, in secret, to keep. That was one of her most darkest secrets that only Diana knew about.

And Mr. Gellert left her the gift, hidden with her new school book order— and a note, telling her, that his father, when he was a boy, somehow took it from his father, and by some miracle, never got caught. He had thought Jayne was worthy enough for it's merit when he apparently found it again in his attic one day, as it had no use in his classroom (because it didn't have the same, normal binding and cover that all books had to have today.) And even more alarmingly so, (or interestingly to Jayne,) was that it had said a name on the cover. Her name, to be exact. And instead of the plain, brown leather casing and gold letters for the lone title, it had a colorful, hand-drawn, illustrated cover. And then her name, under the title. Well, her first name, to be exact. Mr. Gellert wrote explicitly to never show it to anyone. So naturally, it became her ultimate-favorite novel, by choice.

There weren't too many books to choose from at the Library for entertainment, but she still treasured them as such. There were the classics, and some of these ones were her favorites: The Great Gatsby, A Tale of Two Cities, Wuthering Heights, The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn, Oliver Twist, Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland, Charlotte’s Web, To Save A Mockingbird...  And of course, her ultimate favorite— the beloved novel that belonged to her, that she read over and over and over again (in Closed-Door-Time, of course.) And each different time she read it, she found a new meaning within the pages, a new way to look at the story. It was truly the one gift she would protect with her life, hidden and tucked away in her dresser drawer.

Pride and Prejudice... by Jane Austen. This was why she didn't completely hate her given name. Because she shared it with someone as special, as Jane Austen. Jayne believed her to be the original writer, but that was merely a prospect of her impossible imagination. But oh, how she dreamed of one day, meeting someone as handsome and intelligent and... interesting as, Mr. Darcy. Someone who tried to understand her... because everyone else could not. Someone misunderstood by everyone... that she could try and understand.

However, as much time as she spent reading escape fiction, she also quite enjoyed spending her time studying. Though, the subject matter was coincidentally never what was assigned for school. (It was infinite what Jayne could accomplish when she was supposed to be doing something else.) And there were many non-fiction books she loved to explore— specifically, about detail-world history, the human body, medicine, space, electricity, animals, art, sports, and so on...

Jayne loved to learn more than anything in the entire world and that was what she was most especially anticipating about College. Six whole years to learn how to live her Dream Life.

College.

Prepare for your Dream Life.

Your Assignments await!

That was another Advertisement she saw in the paper earlier this week. And it gave her the butterflies. But not the good kind— the bad butterflies.

What Dream Career would be assigned to her? A doctor? A writer? An actress? She would love to live in Holly-Wood town with all of the other actors and actresses. Oh, that would be so divine...

Or maybe she would be a teacher? Her mother believed this would be her destiny, as that was what she was assigned at her age. (She had taught Jayne’s class in second, fourth, sixth, eighth, tenth, and twelfth year, which had been a dramatic source of trauma for Jayne.)

There were so many possibilities for her future and they vivaciously kept her awake at night— imagining and imagining and imagining it all. Regardless, she would find out her Dream Career very soon. And she couldn't wait to start preparing to be the best person fit for her destiny job— researching once she found out her title, yes, she would get right to work!

And if that wasn't enough to make her mind spin, she always fell back on compulsively imagining what her ideal husband would be like...

Or wife... possibly? Though she was quite certain she had only had crushes on boys in Primary school, she was still open for anything that she knew was supposed to be her destiny. 

Though why did she still feel, as if her heart was squeezed in too tight for her chest, and her stomach bubbling with malintentioned butterflies?

Her fate was in sure hands, she reminded herself. And she didn't exactly understand the process behind those hands, or even who those hands belonged to, but regardless of those things, she knew her assignments were going to be correct, for her. As her parents, her friends' parents, her teachers, the television, the newspapers, even Johanna Jensen— everyone— had all put such faith in the system.

Such faith in the system, that was in the duty behind the Life Officers...

Chapter Three: Life Officer Atheism

Friday, 7:04 AM

June 16th

Yes right, the system behind the Life Officers... and their responsibility. Their dutiful responsibility.

Jayne walked into her room and heard the familiar mechanical sound of the security camera in her ceiling room corner when she closed the door. A camera, by obvious law of privacy, would shut down to angle towards the ground when a bedroom door closed or a washroom door closed. Though one was only allowed a certain amount of Closed-Door-Time (CDT, for short) a day, and rightfully so, because a person, truthfully, only needed that time to bathe, change, and use the toilet. (And in Jayne's case, it was also to occasionally read Pride and Predjudice in private.)

And a person's CDT hours were tracked monthly... so if one were spending a lot of CDT in a short period of time, well, then one was carefully reexamined for an explanation of valid reasoning.

But everywhere else outside CDT... the red-eyed cameras lurked and swayed their heads as she passed by. And not just stationary ones, but the drones were what really drove her anxiety. Small, white drones, about the size of a grapefruit— followed her casually throughout her own house... throughout the Schoolhouse... throughout the Gym, at Movie Theater Thursdays, the Library, the Backyard woods, Diana's house, the Football Stadium, Shopping Mall Mondays (those were bi-monthly)— everywhere.

And the Life Officers were always watching. Well, when they were on duty. Obviously, no one knew when that was, so you pretended like they were watching at all times, just to be safe.

They were the masterminds behind Jayne's everyday life. Behind everybody's individual, everyday life. For safety, security, and sanity, was their motto.

Worry less, live more.

Jayne sat on her bed, throwing off her t-shirt, the springs of the mattress cringing the same sound whenever she collapsed back onto it. She couldn't wait to lay in a new bed. To just be somewhere new for once.

As she stepped in her white stockings, and then slipped on her green and silver, checkered-stitched uniform dress, Jayne was imagining who she would still see around in College that was from Pinecone-Paeonia. All she wanted was Diana. Her darling, beautiful best friend for ever and ever, which they had pledged to each other exactly when they were eight. What was she going to do without her? But as one may see it, a life without those purpose-propelling tools of bravery, meant a life without choice. And a life without choice, could be argued as impeccably worse than a rocked, free world of chaos.

But for one to especially realize what a very real, reality of chaos would really look like- a reality, that one may never acknowledge again- they must understand that choice, and options, were inexplicably what drove one’s human nature to sin. To sin, as in ways of pride and envy, propitiated, by their innate need for greed and brewing rapture for wrath. This, which you, Jayne, have never acted upon. Because you don’t know how.

And I hate you entirely.

I hate you beyond comprehendability because you are of such low importance to me, and yet I am still obligated to follow you around, studying you, as you trip about like a lost lab rat. And though I can clearly see the maze from above, as well as had the opportunity to have seen it being designed, my focus on you has dragged me down such a wildly unrealistic path of frantic distraction.

And it’s rather funny... because you know nothing of what you don’t have. What you could have. What we have, here, on this other side? Aren’t you unhappy like me? We have more than you have, why aren’t I happy?

Of course you’re happy. You don’t care. ...Like a dandelion in the wind, you couldn’t care less where you were going, let alone where the wind came from.

And I’ve seen you be, so blindly happy- yes, almost every day at work, for nearly my entire life. You're happy until you get stressed and then you cry. And then you’re happy again. It’s quite funny when you think of it out of context.

You don’t believe in me anymore I know that, but it really has come to bother me as of late. In fact, I find your lack of faith rather disturbing, Jayne. Why don’t you wonder anymore?

You never used to be afraid to speak your mind?

But to briefly explain to you in short language, Jayne- how your world/our world became the way it was- and it won’t be just what you’ve been taught in history class. No, there was so much more.

So here, was the lawfully decided question that changed life as you never knew it, forever:

“ Was the sacrifice of choice, the key to world peace?”

But isn’t that a contradiction all on its own?

What am I doing here, watching you wander about aimlessly without any sense of purpose or direction- when I could be spending my efforts on more important things, like actually protecting civilization. Places like the Plantation of Criminals could surely use my help.

I would serve as an excellent guard there.

And Jayne, how have you become so shallow... to be brainwashed by their microwaveable selling and superficial slogans? You know, I once thought you were smart, like I.

I once thought you were a lot of things... As another part of myself, I suppose. 

But of course, I was a foolish, rose tinted, thirteen year old then. And I have since learned of the ill dangers of letting one’s emotions interfere with work.

I don’t like being ill.

But now, you must be so brainwashed that you have completely forgotten how to think, and all you talk about now is your impending “future dream life.” That you comedically have no control over.

Yes, but I do.

Yes, oh, I do- and oh will you ever so emphatically not enjoy your Career, I can tell you that much. Yes, your cherishable linguistical intellect, unnaturally attained empathy and creative talents could be so easily overlooked.

That’s the bleak beauty of this whole system. Nobody will notice and nobody will care. Nobody will see your confused, deep-dwelling misery behind those falsely lit, compelled eyes because you will hide it. And you will go on pretending like you are happy like the rest, I have no doubt, and will assuredly assume.

And I feel no remorse.

Though this is all hypothetical, of course. Because I will in no way actually get to carry out these threats, considering I am writing this to you in a letter. But I still mean it all the same.

I hate you. I hate you because you do absolutely nothing wrong. Isn’t that terrible? That I have to sit here, year upon year, uselessly monitoring your silly childish adventures... like I were a ghost and actually there with you- as you, ironically run around in your maze-trap-of-a-box-life... doing absolutely nothing right!

Responsibly right, for your life, I mean. You yourself would think the contradiction was quite paramountly poetic. You’re happy one minute, and then crying the next. It’s exhausting, honestly. Why don’t you pay more attention?

Or has your head been so cryptically emptied over time- replaced solely with the pages of your beloved, fictional, CENSORED nonsense, that you have forgotten your favorite question as a child- Why?

...Well, I don’t suppose you could figure it out anyway.

 

So. This letter is a goodbye to you... but it is also a goodbye to me, permanently giving up. I am simply leaving you behind, and everything else about your wishing world. I cannot go on living this way. I need meaning. I need purpose. I need to figure out how to be something important.

What is that like Jayne?
